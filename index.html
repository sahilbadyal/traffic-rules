4-Way Junction Signal Logic

 

 

How the intersection works

  • Each approach has 4 lanes: a protected left-turn lane, two straight-through lanes, and a right-turn lane.
  • Every lane gets its own signal: a left arrow, a through circle, and a right arrow — each cycles red → green → yellow → red independently.
  • Protected left turns go first. Left-lane traffic gets an exclusive green arrow while every other movement on that road holds at red, so left-turners never have to cross oncoming traffic.
  • Once the left arrow clears, the through and right lanes get a green light together — right turns are permitted alongside straight-through traffic, the way "right on green" normally works.
  • The two roads alternate: North–South runs its full left-then-through sequence, then East–West runs the same sequence, then it repeats.
  • Watch the cars: each one brakes to a stop at the white stop line on red, waits, then pulls away only once its own lane's light turns green.
  • The amber car on the south approach demonstrates a permissive (yielded) left turn: its left arrow goes red after the protected phase, but once the plain through-green comes on it's still legal to turn left there — the driver just has to yield to the oncoming through traffic first, which is why it waits noticeably longer before going.
